## Finding 1

**Technique:** Confidence Audit
**Target:** "Patchwork of conflicting state laws" — the core justification for preemption
**Verified:** partially

### The Audit

The framework's central claim is that divergent state AI laws create an unsustainable compliance burden. Classification:

1. **"Patchwork" exists** — Classification: **Evidenced**. California (SB 53, AB 2013, AB 489, SB 243, AB 325), Colorado (AI Act), Texas (RAIGA), and Illinois (HB 3773) all enacted different AI laws effective January 1, 2026. Each imposes different requirements: California mandates training data transparency; Colorado requires impact assessments for high-risk AI; Texas restricts specific harmful uses; Illinois amends employment discrimination law. A company operating in all four states faces genuinely divergent compliance obligations.

2. **"Conflicting" laws** — Classification: **Reasoned**. The laws address different aspects of AI and are largely complementary, not contradictory. A company could comply with all four simultaneously, though at cost. "Conflicting" overstates the legal friction; "overlapping and cumulative" would be more precise.

3. **Burden is unsustainable** — Classification: **Assumed**. No economic impact data, compliance cost estimates, or company testimony is cited in the framework. The assertion is stated as self-evident.

### Verdict

Partially verified. The patchwork is real and the compliance burden is genuine. But the framework overstates the conflict (the laws are more complementary than contradictory) and provides no evidence for the claim that the burden is unsustainable. David Sacks's interstate commerce argument — "trained in state A, inferenced in state B, delivered in state C" — is analytically sound but doesn't prove the existing patchwork is economically harmful, only that it's jurisdictionally complex.

## Finding 3

**Technique:** Frame Audit
**Target:** "Pro-innovation" vs. "deregulatory" framing
**Verified:** no

### The Audit

The framework's dominant frame is "pro-innovation" — AI regulation must be light-touch to maintain American competitiveness. This frame determines the policy direction: state laws are "burdensome," federal standards should be "minimally burdensome," and the goal is to "win the AI race."

Rejecting the frame: restate as "deregulatory." The framework proposes removing existing state regulations without replacing them with federal ones. Under the deregulatory frame, the question changes from "how do we promote innovation?" to "what protections are we removing and what replaces them?" The answer is: nothing replaces them.

Does the answer change? Yes. Under "pro-innovation," preemption is a necessary simplification. Under "deregulatory," preemption is the removal of consumer protections without substitution. The frame is load-bearing — the framework's policy conclusions depend entirely on which frame you adopt.

### Verdict

The "pro-innovation" frame is unexamined and load-bearing. No evidence is presented that state AI laws have reduced innovation, investment, or development. The framework assumes the correlation between regulation and reduced innovation without establishing it.

## Finding 4

**Technique:** Source Trail
**Target:** Copyright position — "training on copyrighted material does not violate copyright"
**Verified:** no

### The Audit

The framework states: the administration "believes that training of AI models on copyrighted material does not violate copyright laws" but "supports allowing the Courts to resolve this issue."

Source trail:
- **Primary authority:** None. The Copyright Office released a report in May 2025 that explicitly declined to take this position, instead presenting arguments on both sides. No court has ruled definitively on AI training as fair use.
- **Industry source:** The position aligns with the AI Progress trade group (Amazon, Anthropic, Google, Meta, Microsoft, Midjourney, OpenAI), who welcomed the language.
- **Counter-authority:** Senators Hawley (R-MO) and Blumenthal (D-CT) introduced legislation the same week to prohibit AI training without permission. The Human Artistry Campaign (SAG-AFTRA, DGA, WGA, IATSE) maintains the opposite position. Trump himself said in July 2025 that paying for all copyrighted content is "not do-able."

Classification: **Political position** stated as legal conclusion. The framework asserts a position that the Copyright Office declined to assert, that courts have not resolved, and that a bipartisan Senate bill directly contradicts.

### Verdict

Not verified as a legal determination. The copyright position is a policy preference presented with the confidence of a legal conclusion. The simultaneous disclaimer ("supports allowing the Courts to resolve this") undermines the assertion's authority — if the administration believed it were settled law, it wouldn't need courts to resolve it.

## Finding 5

**Technique:** Absence Map
**Target:** What's NOT in the framework
**Verified:** yes

### The Audit

What's absent:

- **Enforcement mechanism.** No federal agency is designated, created, or empowered to enforce any provision. No penalties for any violation. No compliance timeline. Compare: EU AI Act Article 99 imposes fines up to 7% of worldwide turnover. This framework imposes nothing.
- **Liability framework.** No provision addresses who is liable when AI causes harm. No safe harbors, no negligence standards, no product liability clarification. The framework removes state protections without establishing federal liability rules.
- **Algorithmic accountability.** No transparency requirements, no audit mandates, no impact assessment obligations. The state laws being preempted (Colorado's AI Act, California's SB 53) contain these; the framework that would replace them does not.
- **Civil rights protections.** No anti-discrimination provisions for AI systems. Illinois's HB 3773 (which this framework would preempt) specifically addresses AI discrimination in employment. The framework proposes no federal substitute.
- **National security safeguards.** Despite citing the AI race with China, no provisions address AI safety testing, frontier model risk assessment, or national security review of AI capabilities. The CSIS reaction noted this gap.
- **Open source governance.** No mention despite open-source AI models being the fastest-growing deployment category.

### Verdict

Verified. The absence of enforcement mechanisms, liability frameworks, and civil rights protections in a document proposing to preempt state laws that contain these provisions is the framework's most consequential feature. The framework proposes to create a federal vacuum, not a federal standard.

# 虎 Tiger — Solution Attack

**Source notice:** Attacking the preemption-first approach as a solution to AI governance challenges. The attack targets the regulatory strategy, not the political intent.

## Finding 1

**Technique:** Inversion Attack
**Target:** Federal preemption without comprehensive federal law
**Burned:** yes

### The Attack

Invert: the federal government cannot actually preempt state laws without "occupying the field." Design the world where this is true:

The Supremacy Clause (Article VI) allows federal law to preempt state law when Congress clearly intends preemption OR when federal regulation is so comprehensive that it occupies the field. This framework explicitly avoids comprehensive federal regulation. Multiple law firms (Goodwin, Ropes & Gray, Gibson Dunn) have noted that the December 2025 executive order faces the same constitutional problem: executive orders cannot preempt state law — only federal legislation can.

The framework asks Congress to preempt state AI laws while simultaneously asking Congress not to pass comprehensive federal AI regulation. This is a constitutional paradox: you can't claim the field is occupied when you've chosen to leave it empty.

As Goodwin's analysis stated: state laws "generally are free to regulate commerce — and even interstate commerce — so long as regulations do not discriminate against out of state actors." None of the target state laws (California, Colorado, Texas, Illinois) discriminate against out-of-state actors.

### Verdict

Burned. The preemption approach fails constitutionally without a comprehensive federal law to anchor it. The framework proposes the conclusion (preemption) without the prerequisite (federal regulation). This isn't a policy disagreement — it's a structural impossibility under existing constitutional doctrine.

## Finding 2

**Technique:** Parallel Universe
**Target:** Alternative approach — federal floor with state discretion above it
**Burned:** yes

### The Attack

The parallel universe: instead of preempting state laws, Congress establishes a federal floor — minimum AI standards that all states must meet — while preserving state authority to impose stricter requirements. This is the model used for environmental regulation (Clean Air Act), consumer protection (FTC Act), and financial regulation (Dodd-Frank).

What does the alternative make easy that the proposal makes hard? Constitutionality — federal floors with state discretion are well-established doctrine. Political viability — 17 Republican governors opposed preemption, but a federal floor preserves their authority. Enforcement — state AGs enforce federal floors alongside federal agencies, multiplying enforcement capacity without federal funding.

What does the proposal make easy that the alternative makes hard? Industry compliance simplicity — one federal standard is genuinely simpler than 50. Regulatory speed — a single point of lobbying influence is more efficient for industry than 50.

### Verdict

Burned. The parallel universe is superior on every dimension except industry lobbying efficiency. The federal floor model is constitutionally tested, politically viable (preserves state authority), and enforcement-practical. The framework chose the approach that is constitutionally dubious, politically rejected (99-1), and enforcement-absent — but simpler for industry.

## Finding 3

**Technique:** Survivor Bias Probe
**Target:** Federal preemption of state technology regulation — where has this worked?
**Burned:** yes

### The Attack

Where has the "preempt state tech regulation" approach succeeded?

- **Telecom (1996 Telecommunications Act):** Preempted state regulation of interstate telecom services. RESULT: decades of litigation over what counts as "interstate," regulatory capture, and broadband deployment gaps. The FCC's net neutrality preemption was itself reversed. The model produced regulatory instability, not regulatory clarity.
- **Internet sales tax (1990s-2010s):** Congress effectively preempted state taxation of internet sales for two decades. RESULT: states lost billions in revenue; when preemption was reversed (Wayfair v. South Dakota, 2018), the patchwork that emerged was the same patchwork preemption was supposed to prevent.
- **National bank preemption (OCC):** Federal banking regulators preempted state consumer protection laws. RESULT: the 2008 financial crisis was partially attributed to preemption of state predatory lending laws that would have constrained the practices that triggered the crisis.

Pattern: federal preemption of state technology regulation has historically produced regulatory vacuums that either get filled by the courts (telecom), get reversed when the costs become visible (sales tax), or contribute to systemic crises when the preempted state laws would have prevented harm (banking).

### Verdict

Burned. The historical pattern predicts that preemption without replacement will produce a regulatory vacuum, a crisis, and overcorrective re-regulation — the same cycle that played out in telecom, internet taxation, and banking. The framework bets against a well-established historical pattern.

## Finding 4

**Technique:** Pre-mortem
**Target:** Framework failure — it's March 2028
**Burned:** yes

### The Attack

It's March 2028. The pre-mortem:

Congress partially enacted the framework in late 2026 — preemption passed narrowly, but without comprehensive federal AI standards (which couldn't get 60 Senate votes). State AI laws (California SB 53, Colorado AI Act, Texas RAIGA, Illinois HB 3773) were nullified.

In 2027, an AI-powered hiring system used by a major employer was found to systematically discriminate against applicants over 50. The system operated across 30 states. Under the pre-preemption regime, Colorado's AI Act would have required impact assessments that would have caught the bias. Illinois's HB 3773 would have provided a state cause of action. Under the post-preemption regime, affected applicants had only Title VII — which doesn't address algorithmic bias, doesn't require impact assessments, and places the burden of proof on the plaintiff to demonstrate discriminatory intent rather than discriminatory effect.

Public outcry produced the "AI Accountability Act of 2028" — omnibus federal legislation more prescriptive than any state law it replaced, including mandatory algorithmic audits, strict liability for AI discrimination, and a new federal AI enforcement agency. The industry that lobbied for preemption now faces stricter regulation than the state patchwork it eliminated.

### Verdict

Burned. Each step in the pre-mortem follows directly from the framework's structural properties: preemption without replacement creates a vacuum, the vacuum enables harm, harm triggers overcorrection. The framework treats regulatory absence as a stable equilibrium — historically, it never is.

## Finding 5

**Technique:** Steel-and-Strike
**Target:** The framework at its strongest
**Burned:** no

### The Attack

Steel-man: the framework is a negotiating position, not a final proposal. Congress will add enforcement mechanisms, liability provisions, and civil rights protections during the legislative process — because it always does. The framework's six prongs provide a structure for those additions. Federal preemption with comprehensive federal standards is the right destination — the framework just omits the standards that will be added through negotiation. The child safety prong demonstrates this: it builds on COPPA's existing federal floor, showing the framework's authors understand the model. The copyright position, while politically explosive, forces a long-overdue legislative resolution. And the "patchwork" problem is real and growing — each month without federal action adds state laws.

Strike: the steeled version holds only if Congress adds the missing federal standards. But the framework explicitly argues for "minimally burdensome" regulation — it's not a skeleton waiting for muscles, it's a design principle of minimal federal intervention. The legislative history confirms this: the December 2025 EO directed the Commerce Secretary to identify "burdensome" state laws, using the same language the framework uses for federal proposals. The framework's authors don't intend for Congress to add comprehensive standards — they intend the vacuum.

However: the steeled version is what would happen politically. Congress won't pass preemption without adding something — the 99-1 vote proved that. The legislative process will transform this framework into something more balanced, or it won't pass at all. The framework itself is DOA; what Congress produces from it may not be.

### Verdict

Survived — but only as a catalyst for a legislative process that will fundamentally transform it. The framework as written is structurally unsound. The legislative process it triggers may not be. The framework's value is not its content but its existence as a White House-branded starting point for congressional negotiation.

## Truth 1

**Technique:** The Uncomfortable Answer

### The Truth

This framework is a lobbying document produced for the AI industry and branded with the White House seal. Its six prongs directly map to the top priorities of the AI Progress trade group (Amazon, Anthropic, Google, Meta, Microsoft, Midjourney, OpenAI): kill state regulation, declare training legal, avoid federal obligations. The question isn't whether the framework reflects industry preferences (it does) but whether it also reflects public interest (it doesn't demonstrate this).

## Finding 3

**Technique:** Compounding Audit
**Target:** Regulatory vacuum trajectory — does it get better or worse with time?
**Farsighted:** no

### The Shadow

**Negative compounding identified.** A regulatory vacuum in AI compounds negatively through two mechanisms:

1. **Capability acceleration:** AI capabilities grow exponentially while governance capacity grows linearly (when it grows at all). Each month of vacuum allows more capable systems to deploy without oversight. The gap between what AI can do and what regulators can assess widens with each advance.

2. **Precedent erosion:** Each company that deploys without obligations creates a baseline. "We've been doing this for two years with no issues" becomes an argument against future regulation. The longer the vacuum persists, the harder it becomes to impose requirements on systems that are already deployed and generating revenue.

3. **Political capital depletion:** Preemption spends enormous political capital. If the resulting vacuum proves harmful, the political capital to pass comprehensive federal regulation has already been spent on preemption. The administration has one chance to shape AI governance — spending it on removing state laws rather than building federal ones is a non-recoverable investment.

### Verdict

Not farsighted. The regulatory vacuum compounds negatively on every dimension — capability gap widens, precedent erodes, political capital depletes. The framework treats the vacuum as a temporary state that will be filled by future legislation. But the compounding dynamics make future legislation both harder to pass and less effective when passed.

## Finding 4

**Technique:** Entanglement Trace
**Target:** Framework's coupling to the December 2025 executive order
**Farsighted:** no

### The Shadow

The framework's legal foundation is the December 11, 2025 executive order ("Ensuring a National Policy Framework for Artificial Intelligence"). Entanglement map:

- **EO directs AG task force** to challenge state laws → if the framework fails in Congress, the EO continues independently → but EOs cannot preempt state law, only federal legislation can → the EO's enforcement mechanism is litigation, not preemption
- **EO directs Commerce Secretary** to identify "burdensome" state laws by March 11, 2026 → this list becomes the preemption target list → but the list is compiled by the executive branch without congressional input → Congress may disagree with the categorizations
- **EO conditions federal funding** on states avoiding "onerous" AI regulations → this is an indirect preemption mechanism (carrot/stick, not legal nullification) → constitutionally fragile under NFIB v. Sebelius (2012) anti-coercion doctrine → states may challenge funding conditions

The critical fragility: the entire framework rests on an executive order that a future administration can revoke on day one. If the framework doesn't become legislation, the policy evaporates with the administration. The framework is building on sand — EO sand that washes away with the next election.

### Verdict

Not farsighted. The framework's foundation (an executive order) is the least durable form of federal policy. Legislation lasts until repealed. EOs last until revoked. Building a national AI governance strategy on an EO that the next administration can delete is structurally fragile. The framework acknowledges this by asking Congress to legislate — but if Congress doesn't act (as the 99-1 vote suggests), the entire structure is temporary.

## Finding 5

**Technique:** Decay Profile
**Target:** How does a deregulatory framework age?
**Farsighted:** no

### The Shadow

Decay properties of deregulatory frameworks in technology:

- **Telecom deregulation (1996):** Decayed as convergence (internet, phone, cable) made the categories obsolete. The deregulatory framework assumed stable technology categories that didn't persist.
- **Financial deregulation (1999-2007):** Decayed as innovation (derivatives, securitization) outpaced the remaining regulatory infrastructure. Culminated in the 2008 crisis.
- **Social media non-regulation (2000s-present):** Section 230 created a regulatory vacuum that persisted until platform harms became politically unsustainable. The vacuum lasted ~20 years before producing serious legislative pressure.

Pattern: deregulatory frameworks in technology decay through the same mechanism: technology advances beyond the assumptions that justified deregulation, harms accumulate, and political pressure produces re-regulation that is typically more restrictive than the regime that was deregulated.

The Trump AI framework has the decay profile of a deregulatory framework in a fast-moving technology sector. Based on historical precedent, the expected lifecycle is: deregulation → harm accumulation → crisis → overcorrective re-regulation. The duration of the deregulatory period varies (7 years for finance, ~20 years for social media), but the endpoint is consistent.

### Verdict

Not farsighted. The framework has the structural decay properties of every prior technology deregulation effort. The question is not whether re-regulation occurs — it's when and how severe. The framework does not account for this cycle, which is the dominant historical pattern for its regulatory category.

## Finding 1

**Technique:** Confidence Audit
**Target:** Interstate commerce argument (Sacks) — constitutional certainty exceeds doctrinal support
**Verified:** no

### The Audit

Classification: **Reasoned, presented as Evidenced.** Sacks frames the interstate commerce argument as constitutionally self-evident. This elides a critical distinction: the federal government's *power* to regulate interstate commerce does not mean states *cannot* regulate it. The 2023 Supreme Court decision in *National Pork Producers Council v. Ross* upheld California's Proposition 12 — a state law regulating out-of-state production standards — rejecting a dormant Commerce Clause challenge. Harvard Law Review (January 2026) warns the strategy risks transforming the dormant Commerce Clause into a blunt deregulatory instrument. The argument also conflates congressional preemption (requires legislation) with executive preemption (which an EO cannot accomplish).

### Verdict

The legal reasoning is stated with constitutional certainty that exceeds the doctrinal support. Classification: reasoned argument presented as settled law.

The 10-year waiver duration is notably long compared to typical 6-24 month cohorts.

# 鼠 Rat — Consequence Map

## Finding 1

**Technique:** Cascade Map
**Target:** Federal preemption fails in Congress → AG litigation → multi-year regulatory ambiguity
**Contained:** no

### The Chain

**First order:** Congress doesn't pass preemption (99-1 precedent). Administration falls back on AG litigation challenging state laws.

**Second order:** Multi-year litigation where state laws remain on the books but face federal challenge. Companies face compliance bifurcation: large firms comply with everything simultaneously; smaller firms pick which jurisdiction to ignore.

**Third order:** A circuit split reaches the Supreme Court around 2028-2029. Until then, the regulatory environment isn't a vacuum — it's a patchwork under active legal assault, which is worse than either a clear federal standard or clear state authority.

### Terminal State

Three to four years of regulatory ambiguity resolved by the Supreme Court in a narrow ruling that settles one question while leaving the broader framework unresolved. The framework designed to create regulatory clarity produces the longest period of regulatory ambiguity in US tech policy history.

## Finding 2

**Technique:** Feedback Loop
**Target:** "Training is legal" signal → creator defensive countermeasures → two-tier content economy
**Contained:** no

### The Chain

**First order:** Framework signals training is legal. AI companies accelerate training during the window of legal ambiguity.

**Second order:** Creators shift strategy — poisoning, watermarking, paywalls at scale. Supply of high-quality, legally clean training data contracts. Feedback loop: the more the signal says unauthorized training is acceptable, the more creators invest in making data unusable.

**Third order:** AI companies offer licensing deals — but only to large rights-holders who can credibly threaten litigation. Independent creators get nothing because they lack litigation leverage. The framework's creator protections produce a two-tier content economy.

### Terminal State

Self-reinforcing cycle where the political signal accelerates both unauthorized training and defensive countermeasures, degrading the open internet as a training resource and consolidating power among entities large enough to negotiate bilateral deals.

## Finding 3

**Technique:** Stakeholder Crossfire
**Target:** Data center permitting — water conflicts are self-limiting
**Contained:** yes

### The Chain

**First order:** Streamlined permitting accelerates data center construction. Winners: hyperscalers and rural communities (jobs, tax revenue). Losers: local residents losing environmental review leverage, municipal water authorities.

**Second order:** Water-stressed communities discover federal permitting overrides local objections. Political backlash is local, from Republican-base constituencies (rural landowners, farmers).

**Third order:** Self-limiting: data center operators have economic incentives to solve water (air-cooling, wastewater recycling). The bottleneck shifts to grid capacity — a physical constraint that throttles buildout more effectively than any regulatory review.

### Terminal State

Contained. Data center expansion hits grid capacity ceiling within 2-3 years. Local water conflicts produce district-level friction but don't aggregate nationally because economic benefits concentrate in the same communities experiencing costs.

## Finding 4

**Technique:** Unintended Consequence
**Target:** Regulatory vacuum → Brussels Effect → US becomes regulatory taker
**Contained:** no

### The Chain

**First order:** Framework creates no federal AI standards. US companies operating globally must comply with EU AI Act (7% turnover penalties are not ignorable).

**Second order:** US companies build compliance infrastructure around EU categories because the US provides no alternative. The EU's risk-based classification becomes the default taxonomy American companies use internally.

**Third order:** When Congress eventually legislates, it faces a market already organized around EU categories. Path of least resistance: adopt EU-compatible definitions because rewriting compliance infrastructure is prohibitively expensive.

### Terminal State

The United States becomes a regulatory taker rather than a regulatory maker. The framework's theory — that no regulation is better than EU-style regulation — fails because global companies need a standard and adopt whatever exists.

## Finding 5

**Technique:** Threshold Effect
**Target:** MAGA coalition fractures at first concentrated AI displacement event
**Contained:** no

### The Chain

**First order:** Framework written by and for the tech-libertarian wing. Workforce prong offers studies, not programs. Populist wing gets rhetoric.

**Second order:** Divide remains manageable as long as AI displacement is distributed across metro areas and doesn't concentrate in single districts. Threshold not yet crossed as of early 2026.

**Third order:** Threshold activates when displacement hits a geographically concentrated, culturally resonant sector — trucking, call centers, back-office finance. A single 2,000+ person displacement event in one district produces a discontinuous political shift.

### Terminal State

The coalition holds until a visible displacement event forces the administration to choose between factions — with no policy tools to offer the populist wing because the framework contains no worker protections beyond research.
